# ink simulation


There are several approaches you can take, each with its own trade-offs in terms of realism and complexity. Here are some options:

	•	**Procedural Path with Variable Stroke Width:**
Use a path (or a series of Bézier/curveVertex points) where the stroke width varies along the path. Add noise or jitter to the width or path to simulate the natural irregularity of ink on paper.

	•	**Particle or Droplet Simulation:**
Represent the stroke as a series of overlapping particles or small shapes (like droplets or splatters) that blend together. Each particle can have a varying opacity and slight randomness in position, size, or color to mimic the diffusion of ink.

	•	**Texture and Blending:**
Apply a paper texture and draw your stroke using a brush (or a custom shape) with alpha blending. By combining a textured brush with a blending mode (like multiply or darken), you can simulate the look of ink seeping into paper fibers.

	•	**Diffusion and Bleed Effects:**
Simulate the behavior of ink spreading on porous paper by using a diffusion algorithm. This might involve iteratively applying a filter or a cellular automata approach to spread color from the stroke’s core into the surrounding area.

	•	**Vector Field Distortion:**
Draw a smooth stroke and then distort it with a vector field or noise function. This can create wavy, organic edges that mimic the uneven absorption of ink.

	•	**Multi-Layered Rendering:**
Render the stroke in multiple passes, varying opacity and line thickness with each pass. Overlapping these layers can add depth and a realistic “wet” appearance as the ink settles on the paper.

Each method can be combined or refined depending on whether you want a stylized look or something more photorealistic. For example, you might start with a procedural path, then overlay a particle system for splatter, and finally apply a diffusion filter to simulate bleed on paper.
