# Arrange the 64 into two blocks of 8 x 4?


Here are five clean, defensible ways to lay the 64 hexagrams out as **two blocks of 8×4 (32 each)**. Pick the scheme that best matches your purpose (study vs. divination vs. design).

**1) King Wen “book split”**

* **Block A (8×4):** Hexagrams **1–32** in King Wen order
* **Block B (8×4):** Hexagrams **33–64** in King Wen order

This mirrors a long-standing way the text was divided historically, keeps the classical pairings (1–2, 3–4, …, 63–64) in close proximity within each block, and preserves the received sequencing.  

**2) Fu Xi (binary) halves**

* **Block A (8×4):** **FX #1–32** (binary 000000→011111)
* **Block B (8×4):** **FX #33–64** (binary 100000→111111)

Ideal for computational or pedagogical uses; you see the binary progression at a glance. (Fu Xi / Shao Yong order is the standard binary enumeration of all 6-bit patterns.)  

**3) Trigram grid split (matrix into two 8×4 slabs)**

Start from the classic **8×8 trigram matrix** (rows = upper trigram, columns = lower trigram). Then split the 8 columns into two sets of four:

* **Block A (8×4):** lower trigrams ☰ ☱ ☲ ☳ across all 8 upper trigrams
* **Block B (8×4):** lower trigrams ☴ ☵ ☶ ☷ across all 8 upper trigrams

You retain exact trigram structure while getting two balanced 8×4 panels-great for teaching the upper×lower composition.  

**4) Mawangdui-inspired octets (yang uppers vs. yin uppers)**

The excavated **Mawangdui** sequence groups the 64 as **eight octets** where each octet shares the **same upper trigram**. Use that logic to split into two blocks:

* **Block A (8×4):** four octets with **yang-family uppers** (e.g., Qian, Dui, Li, Zhen), laid out as 2 rows × 4 cols each, stacked vertically
* **Block B (8×4):** four octets with **yin-family uppers** (e.g., Xun, Kan, Gen, Kun), same layout

Historically grounded alternative that shows systematic organization by upper trigram; visually very tidy in two 8×4 cards.  

**5) Nuclear-family columns (structure-of-4)**

Every hexagram has a **nuclear (mutual) hexagram** built from its inner lines (2-3-4 and 3-4-5). In practice, hexagrams can be grouped into **families of four** around a shared nuclear center. One display recipe:

* Make **8 columns** (one per nuclear center you choose to highlight),
* Stack **4 related hexagrams** per column, giving **8×4** per block, and split the 64 into **two** such family panels.

Useful for advanced study of structure and resonance between figures. (Precise family groupings depend on which nuclear centers you highlight.) 

* **Tradition-first:** use **King Wen 1–32 / 33–64**.  
* **Teaching composition:** use the **trigram grid split** (matrix 8×8 → two 8×4 slabs).  
* **Math/compute:** use **Fu Xi halves**.  
* **Textual history:** use **Mawangdui-inspired** blocks.  
* **Deep structure:** use **nuclear families**.

```
If I were to use repeated calls to I Ching to determine a new order of 64, statistically how many calls would be needed to derive a series containing all 64?

```


This is the classic **coupon collector** problem with n=64.

**Expected calls:** E[T]=n\,H_n=64\,H_{64}\approx \mathbf{303.6} casts to see all 64 at least once.  

Helpful waypoints (same model):

* **Median** ≈ n(\ln n+0.3665) → about **290** casts. (Comes from the Gumbel limit; 0.3665 is the median of the standard Gumbel.)  
* **~95% chance** to have all by ≈ n(\ln n+2.97) → about **456** casts.
* **~99% chance** by ≈ n(\ln n+4.61) → about **560** casts. (Tail behavior P\{T \le n\ln n + c n\}\to e^{-e^{-c}}.)  

Why this applies to the I Ching: with the usual coin or yarrow-stalk methods, each line is yin/yang with **50/50** probability (moving vs static line probabilities differ, but yin vs yang totals stay 1/2 each), and lines are treated as independent-so the **primary hexagram** is effectively **uniform over 64**. That makes the coupon-collector estimate appropriate.  

Caveat: If your generator **isn’t uniform** (e.g., biased mapping, correlation between lines), you’ll need **more** than the figures above. In the non-uniform case, the exact expectation can be written in closed form via an integral over the hexagram probabilities. 


```
Given the randomness implied of this order, how could it be interpreted?

```


If you use repeated I Ching consultations to generate a **new “random” sequence of 64**, the *mathematics* says you’ll eventually get all hexagrams (coupon collector), but the **meaning** depends on how you frame it. There are at least four complementary lenses:

* A **diary of encounters** (divinatory path).
* A **mirror of impermanence** (Daoist/Buddhist insight).
* A **synchronistic text** (Jungian psychology).
* A **generative structure** (art/math).

**1. Traditional divinatory lens**

In divination, each cast is a **moment-specific mirror** of conditions. A whole sequence of 64 generated this way could be seen as a **temporal unfolding**: the order reflects your journey or the world’s shifting state, not a timeless truth. Repetition or clustering of certain hexagrams may be interpreted as **emphatic messages** - themes that recur until acknowledged.

**2. Philosophical/Daoist lens**

The received **King Wen sequence** encodes a cosmology (Heaven–Earth, Difficulty–Folly, Already Fulfilled–Not Yet Fulfilled). A **randomly generated sequence** dissolves that received cosmology. Instead, it foregrounds **contingency** and **impermanence**: change without fixed order. This aligns with Daoist/Buddhist ideas that **any order is provisional** - patterns arise in dependence on conditions, not from an intrinsic plan.

**3. Psychological/Jungian lens**

Jung argued the I Ching works through **synchronicity**: the cast resonates with the questioner’s psyche. Constructing a full sequence by repeated casting could be viewed as a kind of **projective test**: the order you generate will seem to map onto your mental landscape, drawing attention to archetypes you’re primed to see. In this view, “random order” is not meaningless - it is **symbolically charged coincidence**.

**4. Mathematical/artistic lens**

From a math standpoint, the order is just a **random permutation**. Its meaning is that it belongs to the vast set of 64! possible orders (an unimaginably large space). From an art/design standpoint, that arbitrariness is itself expressive: you can use it as a **score**, a **visual sequence**, or a **generative artwork**. John Cage used the I Ching in exactly this way: to remove ego choice and let pattern emerge from chance.
