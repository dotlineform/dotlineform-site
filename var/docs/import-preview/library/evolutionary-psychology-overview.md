## Imported Source Text

Core Principles

- Natural selection applies to cognition: psychological traits can be adaptations shaped by selection.
- Functional specialization: the mind comprises many problem-specific mechanisms (often called “modules”).
- Environment of Evolutionary Adaptedness (EEA): many mechanisms evolved in hunter–gatherer contexts; modern life can create mismatches (e.g., sugar preference).

Good primers: Stanford Encyclopedia overview; Cosmides & Tooby’s Primer.

Key Research Areas

- Mating & attraction: cross-cultural regularities and sex-differentiated strategies.
- Parental investment: different reproductive costs shape strategies (Trivers 1972).
- Cooperation & morality: kin selection, reciprocal altruism, and cheater detection.
- Emotion as adaptation: fear, disgust, jealousy, love as fitness-relevant programs.

Strengths

- Unifies psychology with evolutionary biology.
- Generates testable, cross-cultural predictions.
- Explains broad regularities in human behavior.

Critiques & Debates

- Adaptationism: over-attributing traits to selection vs. byproducts/culture.
- “Just-so” hypotheses: prehistoric narratives can be difficult to falsify.
- Culture & learning: concern that cultural variation is underweighted.
- Scientific status debates: boundary questions with sociobiology and philosophy of science.

Links to Project Themes

- Memory & future planning: episodic memory may have evolved for prospective simulation (planning), aligning with your “remembering forward” theme.
- Select – Change – Reconstruct: adaptive attention (select), plasticity/learning (change), and reconstructive recall (reconstruct) fit EP’s functional lens.
- Processing app notes: when designing interfaces or visualizations about memory, consider evolved biases (e.g., salience for faces, threats, kin signals).
